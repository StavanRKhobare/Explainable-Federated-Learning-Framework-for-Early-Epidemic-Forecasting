Explainable Federated Learning Framework for Early Epidemic Forecasting is a privacy-preserving artificial intelligence framework that enables multiple healthcare institutions to collaboratively predict dengue and malaria outbreaks without sharing sensitive patient data. By integrating federated learning, temporal graph neural networks, and automated text anonymization, the system provides actionable outbreak forecasts with built-in explanations, directly supporting SDG 3.3 (combating epidemics) and SDG 3.8 (universal health coverage).
Dengue and malaria collectively affect over 400 million people annually, yet current surveillance systems detect outbreaks only after widespread transmission, missing critical opportunities for early intervention.
Privacy regulations prevent hospitals from sharing patient data, creating isolated silos that hide cross-institutional outbreak patterns a gap this framework bridges through federated learning without compromising data sovereignty.

Clear statement of the problem: Existing outbreak prediction systems for dengue and malaria are limited by centralized learning approaches, lack of privacy preservation, insufficient spatio-temporal modeling, and minimal explainability. These limitations make it difficult to generate early, accurate, and interpretable outbreak forecasts using distributed healthcare data from multiple institutions. Therefore, there is a need for a unified framework that can securely integrate multi-source data and provide reliable predictions for timely public health intervention.
Context of the Problem: Vector-borne diseases like dengue and malaria continue to pose a major global health challenge, particularly in densely populated and climate-sensitive regions. The spread of these diseases is influenced by multiple factors such as weather conditions, population density, hospital case trends, and clinical observations over time. Traditional surveillance approaches primarily rely on historical case counts and manual reporting, which often fail to capture temporal and spatial spread patterns at an early stage.
Moreover, a large portion of clinically useful information exists in unstructured physician notes and hospital records, which is frequently underutilized by conventional analytical models. Existing AI-based forecasting approaches often lack privacy preservation, interpretability, and fairness considerations, making them less suitable for real-world deployment in healthcare environments.

Significance of Addressing the Problem: Explain Failure to address this problem can lead to delayed outbreak warnings, increased infection rates, higher mortality, and severe strain on healthcare infrastructure. Without an effective predictive surveillance framework, health authorities may be forced to act only after outbreaks have escalated, reducing the effectiveness of containment measures such as vector control, resource allocation, and public advisories.
The major gaps that arise if this issue is not addressed include:
Delayed outbreak response, leading to preventable spread of disease 
Lack of collaboration across healthcare institutions due to privacy constraints 
Underutilization of critical clinical data, especially unstructured notes 
Limited trust in AI predictions because of poor explainability 
Potential healthcare disparities, as underserved regions may receive less accurate predictions 
Addressing this problem is therefore essential for enabling proactive, privacy-preserving, and explainable epidemic forecasting, which can support timely decision-making and improve public health outcomes.

Main Objective: To develop a privacy-preserving and explainable AI framework for early forecasting of dengue and malaria outbreaks using spatio-temporal healthcare data.
Specific Objectives:
To design a spatio-temporal graph-based model for predicting outbreak risk across regions and time periods.
To enable privacy-preserving collaborative learning across multiple healthcare institutions using federated learning.
To incorporate explainability mechanisms that provide interpretable outbreak predictions for public health officials.
To integrate multi-source data such as case records, environmental factors, and hospital trends for improved forecasting accuracy.
Expected Contributions to the Field:
A unified framework for early epidemic forecasting and interpretability.
Improved decision support for proactive public health intervention and resource planning.
A scalable approach that advances existing work in AI-driven disease surveillance systems.
